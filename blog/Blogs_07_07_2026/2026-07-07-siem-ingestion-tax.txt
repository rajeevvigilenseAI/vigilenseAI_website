<h2>What is SIEM ingestion tax?</h2>
<p>The SIEM ingestion tax is a pricing strategy used by legacy Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) and Managed Detection and Response (MDR) providers where the customer is billed based on the volume of data ingested into the vendor's platform. Whether measured in Gigabytes per day (GB/day) or Events Per Second (EPS), this model essentially penalizes a company for its own growth and digital activity.</p>

<p>Beyond the simple definition, this "tax" represents a fundamental misalignment between a vendor's profit and a customer's security. In a traditional cloud SIEM, every new employee, every cloud migration, and every increase in network traffic results in a higher bill. This forces IT teams to perform "log surgery," choosing which data sources are "important enough" to monitor and which are too expensive, often leaving critical gaps that sophisticated attackers use to hide their movements.</p>

<h3>What is Log Filtering?</h3>
<p>Log filtering is the practice of intentionally excluding certain data types from a security system to avoid high ingestion costs. While it saves money, it often removes the context needed to identify "low and slow" advanced persistent threats (APTs).</p>
</div>

<p>The SIEM ingestion tax is important because it dictates the "visibility ceiling" of an organization. Organizations that lack full visibility because of cost constraints take longer to identify and contain these breaches.</p>

<p>When you are taxed on data, security becomes a luxury. For midsize businesses, this is particularly devastating. Attackers know that smaller firms often have "blind spots" in their DNS logs or endpoint telemetry because those logs are high-volume and expensive to ingest. <h2 id="how-it-works">How does SIEM ingestion tax work?</h2>
<p>The mechanics of the SIEM ingestion tax are simple but punishing. Most cloud-native SIEMs (like Sentinel or Splunk) require you to move your logs from your local servers, cloud environments (AWS/Azure), and SaaS apps into their proprietary cloud storage. </p>

<p>Here is the breakdown of the "tax" layers:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Data Transfer Fees:</strong> You pay your cloud provider (like AWS) to move data out of your environment (Egress fees).</li>
<li><strong>Ingestion Fees:</strong> You pay the SIEM provider a set rate per GB to "process" and "index" that data.</li>
<li><strong>Retention Fees:</strong> You pay a monthly fee to keep that data searchable for 30, 90, or 365 days.</li>
</ul>

<p>As your company adopts more cloud services, your log volume typically grows by 20% to 30% annually. This means even if the vendor doesn't raise their prices, your bill increases every year simply because your business is active. This creates a "predictability crisis" for CISOs and CFOs who cannot accurately forecast their security spend.</p>

<div>
<h3>What is Egress Pricing?</h3>
<p>Egress pricing refers to the fees charged by cloud service providers for moving data out of their network. This is the "hidden" first half of the SIEM ingestion tax that many companies overlook.</p>
</div>

<h2 id="implementation-steps">How do you implement a zero-ingestion security model?</h2>
<p>Transitioning away from the ingestion tax requires a shift in architecture. Here is the 5-step process to reclaim your budget.</p>

<h3>Step 1: Audit Your Current Data Pipeline</h3>
<p>Identify every data source currently being sent to your SIEM. Note the volume (GB/day) and the associated cost. Identify which sources you are currently <i>not</i> sending because they are too expensive (usually DNS or high-volume network logs).</p>

<h3>Step 2: Adopt a Data-Resident AI Layer</h3>
<p>Instead of moving logs to the AI, bring the AI to the logs. Deploy an AI-powered detection engine, like Vigilense AI, that connects directly to your existing data lakes (like Snowflake, S3, or Azure Monitor) or local storage. This ensures your data stays in your infrastructure.</p>

<h3>Step 3: Define Your "Detection Logic" Locally</h3>
<p>Set up automated SOC workflows that run on your data in situ. This involves configuring the AI to look for patterns of lateral movement, credential theft, and data exfiltration without needing to "index" the data in a secondary location.</p>

<h3>Step 4: Automate Response Actions</h3>
<p>Connect your detection layer to your response tools (EDR, Firewall, IAM). When the AI identifies a threat locally, it should be able to trigger a block or isolation command immediately, reducing the "Mean Time to Respond" (MTTR).</p>

<h3>Step 5: Sunset the Legacy Ingestion SIEM</h3>
<p>Once your data-resident model is validated, begin scaling back your legacy SIEM ingestion. Start with the highest-volume, lowest-value logs and move toward a full "Zero Ingestion" posture. Redirect the saved budget into proactive threat hunting or better endpoint protection.</p>

<h2 id="common-mistakes">What are common SIEM ingestion tax mistakes?</h2>
<p>Many organizations try to manage the tax rather than eliminate it. This usually leads to one of these three common errors:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Over-Filtering:</strong> Removing logs that seem "useless" (like successful logins) but are actually vital for reconstruction during a forensic investigation.</li>
<li><strong>Short Retention Periods:</strong> Keeping data for only 7–14 days to save on storage. Since the average breach takes over 200 days to detect, this makes forensic analysis impossible.</li>
<li><strong>Ignoring Egress Costs:</strong> Focusing only on the SIEM's per-GB price while ignoring the massive bill from AWS/Azure for moving that data out of the region.</li>
<li><strong>Building "DIY" Data Lakes:</strong> Trying to build a custom Hadoop or ELK stack to avoid fees, only to realize the labor cost of maintaining it is higher than the original tax.</li>
<li><strong>Assuming "Cloud-Native" Means Free:</strong> Believing that using a provider's own SIEM (like Azure Sentinel) is free. While the integration is easy, the ingestion and retention costs remain high.</li>
</ul>
